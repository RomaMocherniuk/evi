## Task 2 — Multiple Choice (Questions 7–11)

### Text: The Science of Bilingualism

For much of the twentieth century, bilingualism was viewed with suspicion in educational circles. Early studies suggested that children exposed to two languages simultaneously developed each one more slowly than monolingual peers, leading some educators to advise parents to choose a single language for home use. More recent research has overturned this view comprehensively.

Neuroscientific studies using brain-imaging technology reveal that managing two languages exercises the brain's executive control system — the network responsible for filtering distractions, switching between tasks, and resolving cognitive conflicts. Bilinguals, particularly those who have used two languages regularly from childhood, show measurably better performance in tasks requiring sustained attention and mental flexibility. Crucially, these effects extend beyond linguistic tasks into domains such as mathematics, problem-solving, and navigation.

The cognitive benefits of bilingualism appear to intensify with use. Adults who continue to speak both languages daily show delayed onset of dementia symptoms compared to monolinguals with equivalent education and socioeconomic backgrounds — an average delay of four to five years in several landmark studies. Researchers attribute this to what they call "cognitive reserve": the brain's accumulated capacity to compensate for neurological damage.

However, not all researchers are convinced. A minority of studies have failed to replicate the bilingual advantage in laboratory conditions, leading some scientists to argue that the benefits may be overstated or context-dependent. Cultural, socioeconomic, and motivational variables are difficult to isolate in studies of this kind, and publication bias — the tendency to publish positive findings over null results — may have skewed the evidence base.
